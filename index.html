Exploring Factors Influencing a Country's Happiness Score: A Data Analysis Project

In this project, we aimed to explore whether there are particular factors that influence a country's happiness score more than others, and how the data looked at the end of 2020 due to the pandemic. To address this, the project had four main research questions:

1. Is there a correlation between the happiness score of a country and its life expectancy in 2020, and whether there are any patterns or clusters in the data?

2. Is there a relationship between a country's GDP per capita and its happiness score in 2020?

3. Is there a correlation between perceived government corruption and happiness score for countries in 2020?

4. How does the happiness scores of various parts of the world compare through the years of the dataset?

To answer these research questions, we combined eight years of survey data from separate files into one CSV file to eliminate extraneous variables and find commonalities. The data required significant amount of cleaning and manipulation because it was not collected in the same way for each year. In order to visually see how the top 10 ranks compared over time, we created a unique rank variable for the bump chart in Tableau and analyzed changes over time and rank. We then moved to the original research questions and analyzed the correlations between individual variables and the happiness score through scatterplots. Throughout our entire process, we found that countries in the Western European region made up a majority of the happiest countries and had longer life expectancies, higher GDP per capita, etc., for all the years analyzed, even during COVID. We figured that these findings could provide valuable insights for policy makers to emulate the characteristics (policies, public health law implementations, access to education) of these countries to increase their country's happiness.

About the World Happiness Report Dataset

The World Happiness Report is an annual publication that ranks countries by their level of happiness, based on factors such as income, social support, freedom, trust, and life expectancy. The report aims to provide policymakers with information to improve public policies and societal well-being.

The World Happiness Report dataset, available on Kaggle, contains data from the 2015-2022 editions of the report. It includes information on happiness scores, factors contributing to happiness, and other socio-economic and demographic indicators for 170+ countries. The dataset can be used to analyze trends and patterns in happiness, explore the relationship between happiness and various factors, and compare happiness across countries and regions.

This dataset is an excellent resource for researchers, policymakers, and anyone interested in understanding happiness and well-being at a global level. With this dataset, you can perform your analysis, generate insights, and develop data-driven strategies to promote happiness and well-being worldwide.

The Bump Chart graph displays the changes over time in the ranking and happiness score of the top 10 happiest countries in 2015. Most of the countries remained within the top 10 ranking throughout the years, with slight variations in their happiness score. However, two countries, Australia and Canada, experienced a decline in both their ranking and happiness score over the past years. Based on this observation, we can conclude that while most of the top 10 happiest countries in 2015 have maintained their high levels of happiness, some countries have experienced a decline in their happiness score and ranking. It would be interesting to investigate in future research the reasons behind these changes and whether they are related to internal factors, such as changes in government policies or economic conditions, or external factors, such as global events or changes in international relations.
